# Safety Model

## Overview

Synapse Optical is being designed around operational safety, deterministic validation, and human-controlled workflow execution.

The platform is intended to assist engineering teams by improving operational consistency, accelerating troubleshooting workflows, and reducing repetitive manual tasks while maintaining human oversight at every stage of the execution lifecycle.

Operational safety is treated as a primary architectural requirement rather than an optional feature.

---

# Core Safety Principles

## Deterministic Validation First

Structured validation logic and deterministic workflow checks take priority over AI-generated assumptions.

Operational workflows are designed to validate prerequisites, identify conflicts, verify dependencies, detect unsafe conditions, and reduce configuration inconsistency before any production-impacting action is executed.

In practice, this means all device commands are generated by server-side templates — never by the AI layer. The AI system outputs structured semantic intent; templates translate that intent into vendor-specific configuration. This separation ensures that even as AI assistance evolves, command generation remains predictable, testable, and auditable.

---

## Operational Classification: Read-Only vs Change-Capable

Every feature in Synapse Optical is classified at design time as either read-only or change-capable. This classification is an architectural requirement, not a runtime check.

**Read-only operations** — diagnostics, log analysis, configuration snapshots, policy analysis, VPN troubleshooting — require no approval gate and carry no execution risk. They may run freely and are designed to be safe to invoke at any time.

**Change-capable operations** — interface configuration, VPN commissioning, NAT policy changes, address object management, routing protocol configuration — require a complete operational lifecycle: deterministic change plan generation, risk assessment, human approval, controlled execution, post-change verification, and a rollback record.

If the classification of a new feature is ambiguous, it is treated as change-capable. Safety classification escalates; it never downgrades.

---

## Change Lifecycle and Human Approval Gate

Synapse Optical is being designed around a bounded change lifecycle. No production-impacting action executes without an explicit operator approval event.

Change-capable workflows follow a strict state sequence:

```
pending_review → approved → executing → verifying → completed
               ↘ rejected
                            ↘ failed → rolled_back
```

| Stage | Description |
|---|---|
| `pending_review` | Change plan generated with risk assessment attached; awaiting human review |
| `approved` | Operator explicitly approved; execution may begin |
| `executing` | Vendor commands being applied to the device under controlled conditions |
| `verifying` | Post-execution checks running against the live device |
| `completed` | All verification checks passed; change is confirmed live |
| `rejected` | Operator rejected at review; no execution occurred |
| `failed` | Execution or verification failed; rollback initiated |
| `rolled_back` | Rollback completed; device returned to pre-change state |

No state transitions are permitted outside this sequence. A change cannot execute without an explicit approval event. Verification cannot be skipped for change-capable workflows.

The change lifecycle applies equally to AI-assisted natural language requests and to deterministic guided workflow requests. Both paths enter the same approval pipeline.

---

## Risk Assessment Before Execution

Every change-capable workflow generates a risk assessment before it is presented for human review. Risk scoring evaluates the proposed change against declarative, vendor-specific safety rules before any operator approval is requested.

Risk assessment is designed to:

- surface high-risk change characteristics before execution
- produce escalation warnings for changes that exceed risk thresholds
- provide operators with risk context during the approval review
- create a pre-execution risk record as part of the change audit trail

Risk scoring runs deterministically against structured rule definitions. It is never evaluated by the AI layer. The AI system has no influence over risk classification or approval gating.

---

## AI as a Bounded Assistance Layer

AI functionality operates within four bounded, advisory-only roles:

**1. Operational intent interpretation**
The AI system interprets natural language operational requests and outputs structured semantic intent — a fixed-action classification with associated parameters. It does not output device commands, CLI syntax, XPath strings, or REST payloads. The structured output feeds into a deterministic template pipeline that generates the actual change plan.

**2. Log and diagnostic analysis**
The AI system receives operational log data and a natural language query, and outputs a plain-language summary, likely responsible rule, and recommended next actions. Any suggested follow-up action is presented to the operator as an advisory hint — it is never auto-submitted to the change pipeline.

**3. Workflow prerequisite warnings**
The AI system reviews available configuration context before a guided workflow begins and outputs advisory warnings and suggested checks. These findings are displayed to the operator for consideration. A failed prerequisite warning never blocks workflow execution — that decision remains with the operator.

**4. Health assessment executive summary**
After a Firewall Health Assessment run completes, the AI system receives a sanitized payload — severity counts, category scores, and a short list of top findings — and produces a plain-language executive narrative. It never receives raw configuration data, credentials, or CLI output. Detection, scoring, and per-finding evidence are 100% deterministic; the AI contributes only the narrative layer. If this call fails or the AI is unavailable, the assessment run still completes successfully with no executive summary attached.

In each role, AI output is advisory. AI does not generate device commands, does not approve changes, does not produce assessment findings or scores, and does not execute production-impacting actions.

---

## Post-Change Verification

Post-change verification is a first-class lifecycle stage, not an optional check. Every change-capable workflow defines its verification requirements before implementation is considered complete.

After a change executes, the platform runs structured verification checks against the live device to confirm the intended outcome. Verification results — including per-check pass/fail status, the specific resource verified, and supporting command output — are retained as part of the permanent change record.

If verification fails, the platform initiates rollback where supported and marks the change as failed. Verification evidence is stored alongside the change plan and execution record to support post-incident review.

---

## Vendor-Aware Validation

Operational workflows adapt to vendor platform, operating system version, licensing constraints, supported configuration capabilities, and cryptographic support limitations.

This helps reduce invalid or unsupported configuration generation across heterogeneous environments and is designed to catch platform-specific constraint violations before a change enters the approval pipeline.

---

## Auditability and Operational Traceability

Operational workflows are designed with auditability as a primary concern.

The audit record for every change-capable operation includes:

- the full deterministic change plan
- the pre-execution risk assessment
- the approving operator identity
- the execution method and transport selection
- per-step execution results
- post-change verification results, including per-check evidence and raw command output
- rollback records where applicable

Read-only operations are logged for operational visibility. Change-capable operations produce a complete, immutable audit record across the full lifecycle.

---

## RBAC and Privilege Separation

Planned privilege controls for multi-tenant and enterprise deployments include:

- role-based access control with least-privilege role assignment
- separation between operators who submit changes and operators who approve them
- credential isolation between device access and platform authentication
- per-tenant data separation
- audit attribution to individual operators across the full change lifecycle

These controls are being designed into the platform's promotion path from proof-of-concept to production deployment. The data model is structured to support enforcement without schema changes.

---

# Development Stage

Synapse Optical is an active proof-of-concept. The core change lifecycle, deterministic command generation, risk scoring, post-change verification, and AI boundary controls described in this document are implemented in the current PoC.

Some controls — including full RBAC enforcement, TLS certificate validation, multi-tenancy row-level security, and production credential management — are documented as planned promotion requirements. PoC shortcuts in these areas are explicitly tracked and are known preconditions for production deployment, not unacknowledged gaps.

The platform is being developed iteratively with a clear distinction between what is built, what is tracked for promotion, and what is aspirational.
